### SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

(a) **COMPARATIVE INEFFICIENCY OF UNITED STATES MILITARY SPENDING.** The United States dedicates a substantial portion of its national resources to military expenditures, yet this investment does not correlate with superior outcomes in key public welfare metrics when compared to peer nations. The United States ranks 27 out of 28 OECD countries in military spending efficiency.

(b) **DISPARITY IN SPENDING AND OUTCOMES.** In 2026, the United States spends approximately $2052 per capita on its military. Despite this high level of spending, the life expectancy in the United States is 76.93 years. This contrasts sharply with top-performing OECD countries such as Switzerland, which spends $389 per capita with a life expectancy of 83.37 years; Japan, spending $400 per capita with a life expectancy of 84.53 years; and Sweden, spending $565 per capita with a life expectancy of 82.9 years.

(c) **THE EFFICIENCY GAP.** The current military spending of the United States represents an "overspend ratio" of 5.3 times the minimum level ($389 per capita) needed to achieve comparable national well-being outcomes observed in highly efficient nations. This indicates a significant opportunity for reallocation of resources without compromising national security, by adopting more efficient institutional structures and policy mechanisms.

### SECTION 3. PURPOSE.

The purpose of this Act is to systematically enhance the well-being of United States citizens by:
(1) Achieving national well-being outcomes comparable to top-performing countries such as Switzerland, Japan, and Sweden, while reducing per-capita military spending from $2052 toward the efficient floor of $389.
(2) Implementing evidence-based healthcare reforms modeled on cost-effective systems like Singapore's, to improve health outcomes and reduce per-capita healthcare expenditures.
(3) Establishing a Universal Basic Income (UBI) funded by reallocated savings, to directly improve economic stability, health, and social welfare for all citizens.
(4) Reforming federal health research funding to prioritize pragmatic clinical trials, accelerating the development of actionable, cost-effective medical interventions.
(5) Ensuring rigorous, transparent, and continuous evaluation of these reforms through a hard sunset provision, preventing the perpetuation of ineffective policies.

### SECTION 7. EVALUATION AND SUNSET PROVISIONS.

##### SEC. 701. BASELINE MEASUREMENT.

Within 90 days of enactment of this Act, the Government Accountability Office (GAO) shall establish baseline measurements for the following two metrics:
(a) **Real after-tax median household income:** (Source: U.S. Census Bureau, Current Population Survey Annual Social and Economic Supplement (CPS ASEC), and Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)).
(b) **Median healthy life expectancy at birth (HALE):** (Source: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS)).

##### SEC. 702. REVIEW CADENCE.

The GAO shall conduct a biennial review of this Act. Every 2 years following the establishment of baselines, the GAO shall re-measure both metrics specified in Section 701 and compare the results to the baseline. The findings of each review shall be published publicly and submitted to Congress and the President.

##### SEC. 703. TARGET THRESHOLDS.

(a) **Expected Improvements:** Within 5 years of full implementation (end of Year 5), this Act is expected to achieve:
    (1) A sustained average annual increase of **+0.5 percentage points** in real after-tax median household income growth.
    (2) An increase of **+0.3 years** in median healthy life expectancy at birth.
(b) **Minimum Acceptable Thresholds:** The minimum acceptable thresholds for the biennial review are 50% of the expected improvements for each metric. That is, an average annual increase of +0.25 percentage points in real after-tax median household income growth and an increase of +0.15 years in median healthy life expectancy at birth within 5 years.

##### SEC. 704. HARD SUNSET.

If, by the second biennial review (end of Year 4 of full implementation), **BOTH** the real after-tax median household income and the median healthy life expectancy at birth fail to meet their respective **MINIMUM acceptable thresholds** as defined in Section 703(b), this Act shall automatically expire, and all programs established hereunder shall be terminated, unless renewed by a supermajority vote (two-thirds majority in both the House of Representatives and the Senate). This provision ensures accountability and prevents the perpetuation of ineffective legislation.

##### SEC. 705. DATA COLLECTION MANDATE.

To facilitate comprehensive evaluation, the following agencies are mandated to collect and publicly publish relevant data annually:
(a) **Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS):** Annually publish real after-tax median household income data, including breakdowns by quintile and demographic groups.
(b) **Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) / National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS):** Annually publish median healthy life expectancy at birth data, including breakdowns by demographic groups and key health indicators.
(c) **Office of Management and Budget (OMB):** Annually publish detailed per-capita spending data for military, healthcare, and all federally funded research, disaggregated by program and mechanism.
(d) **U.S. Census Bureau:** Annually publish data on poverty rates and food insecurity.
(e) **Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ):** Annually publish data on preventable hospitalization rates (PQIs) and average procedure costs for common elective services.
(f) All mandated data must be publicly accessible in machine-readable formats.

##### SEC. 706. BENCHMARK JURISDICTIONS.

The GAO's biennial review shall include a comparative analysis of the United States' performance on real after-tax median household income and median healthy life expectancy at birth against the following benchmark jurisdictions: Switzerland, Japan, Sweden, Singapore, Canada, and Norway. If these benchmark countries continue to outperform the United States on both metrics, the review must include a detailed explanation of potential contributing factors and recommend specific adjustments to this Act.
